Plate Classification
Stimulant Class
Stimulants are substances that increase activity in the central nervous system, producing heightened arousal, energy, focus, and in some cases euphoria. The class includes compounds ranging from caffeine and MDMA to amphetamines and cocaine. Common examples in festival environments include MDMA, amphetamine, methamphetamine, cocaine, and 2C-B.

This plate represents the stimulant class as a network of cascading signals — neural pathways activated, amplified, and sustained beyond their ordinary duration.
Mechanism Snapshot
How They Act
Most stimulants work by increasing the availability of monoamine neurotransmitters — primarily dopamine, serotonin, and norepinephrine — in the synaptic cleft. They do this through one or more mechanisms: blocking reuptake transporters, stimulating release, or inhibiting breakdown enzymes.

The result is sustained activation of reward, arousal, and motivation circuits — producing the characteristic heightening of energy, confidence, and sensory experience the class is known for.
Cultural Context
Stimulants in Festival Culture
Stimulants have been entwined with dance culture for decades. MDMA emerged alongside early rave culture in the late 1980s. Amphetamines preceded it. The relationship reflects something specific about the environment: festival conditions — duration, heat, physical exertion, social intensity — are physiologically demanding, and stimulants interact with those demands in ways that create both the desired experience and the elevated risk.

Understanding this class means understanding why festival-specific harm reduction guidance differs from general clinical guidance.
Risk Awareness
Key Safety Considerations
Hyperthermia is the leading cause of stimulant-related festival deaths. The combination of stimulant-induced thermogenesis, physical exertion, and ambient heat creates dangerous heat accumulation — particularly with MDMA.

Hyponatraemia (dangerously low sodium) occurs when people over-hydrate with water alone, often driven by misguided harm reduction advice. Sip fluids; do not chug.

Cardiovascular strain is a compounding risk for anyone with underlying cardiac conditions. Re-dosing significantly increases risk and extends the physiological burden.
Harm Reduction Notes
Practical Intelligence for Safer Decisions
Temperature. Take breaks from dancing. Seek shade or cool areas regularly. Overheating is the primary mechanism of stimulant-related harm in festival environments — not overdose in the traditional sense.

Hydration. For active dancing: approximately 500ml water per hour. Do not exceed this. Electrolyte drinks are preferable to plain water in extended situations.

Dosing. Start low. Avoid re-dosing entirely if possible. The second dose does not restore the first experience; it extends duration and compounds cardiovascular and thermal load.

Combinations. Stimulant combinations — particularly MDMA with cocaine or amphetamine — multiply cardiac and thermal risk nonlinearly. The Interaction Matrix provides specific guidance per combination.

Reagent testing. Press contamination with novel psychoactive substances (NPS) or fentanyl analogues is real. Reagent testing kits reduce — not eliminate — this risk. Testing services at events provide more reliable analysis.

Wingmate protocol. Check in. If someone stops dancing, becomes pale, stops sweating despite heat, or becomes confused — these are red-flag indicators. Seek the medical or harm reduction team immediately.
