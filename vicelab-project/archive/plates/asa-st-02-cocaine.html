Scientific Record

Pharmacological Profile

Class Stimulant Tropane alkaloid
Scientific Name Benzoylmethylecgonine
Common Names Cocaine · Coke · Blow · Charlie · Snow · Benzoylmethylecgonine
Primary Mechanism Monoamine reuptake inhibitor. Blocks dopamine transporter (DAT), norepinephrine transporter (NET), and serotonin transporter (SERT) simultaneously. Prevents reuptake of dopamine, norepinephrine, and serotonin into the presynaptic neuron. Net effect: sustained and elevated monoamine concentration in the synaptic cleft.
Local Anaesthetic Blocks voltage-gated sodium channels. Secondary mechanism distinct from CNS stimulation.
Half-Life 0.7 – 1.5 hours (plasma, intranasal route)
Onset Insufflated: 3–5 min · Smoked (freebase): 8–15 sec · Intravenous: 15–30 sec
Duration Insufflated: 45–90 min · Smoked: 20–40 min
Primary Risks Cardiovascular Hyperthermia Compulsive Re-dosing Adulterants / Levamisole Cardiac Arrhythmia
Cocaine-induced myocardial infarction is documented in young, otherwise healthy individuals. Levamisole contamination (commonly 70–80% of supply) causes agranulocytosis with chronic use.
Interactions Alcohol (Cocaethylene) MAOIs MDMA Stimulants
Concurrent alcohol use produces cocaethylene — a metabolite with greater cardiotoxicity and extended half-life than cocaine alone.

Field Intelligence
HARM REDUCTION NOTES ASA-ST-02
Evidence-first · Non-judgemental · Operational
Cardiovascular Load
Cocaine significantly increases heart rate, blood pressure, and cardiac oxygen demand. Anyone with a known cardiac condition should treat this as high-risk. Exertion, heat, and concurrent stimulant use compound cardiac strain multiplicatively. If experiencing chest pain, palpitations, or shortness of breath — stop, rest, seek medical attention.
Dosing Behaviour
The short duration creates a compulsive re-dosing pattern. Each additional dose escalates cardiovascular risk non-linearly. There is no safe re-dosing ceiling — the body's warning signals are pharmacologically suppressed. If a line feels different from usual — stop. This is an adulterant signal.
Test Before You Use
Cocaine supply is routinely adulterated. Levamisole (immunosuppressant) is present in approximately 70–80% of street cocaine. Fentanyl cross-contamination has increased significantly since 2020 in some markets. Reagent testing (Scott, Mecke) and fentanyl test strips are not infallible but materially reduce risk.
Nasal Care
Insufflation damages nasal mucosa progressively. Use your own clean surface and equipment. Rinse nasal passages with saline after use. Do not share implements — blood-borne pathogen transmission risk. Persistent nosebleeds, difficulty breathing, or structural nasal changes warrant medical review.
Alcohol Interaction
Cocaine and alcohol are commonly combined. The liver produces cocaethylene — a third psychoactive compound with greater cardiotoxicity than either substance alone, and a longer elimination half-life. This combination meaningfully extends the cardiovascular risk window.
Overheating
Cocaine is thermogenic — it raises core body temperature. Combined with dancing, warm environments, and dehydration, this creates real hyperthermia risk at festivals. Take regular breaks. Get to a cool area. Sip water. If someone is confused, stops sweating despite heat, or loses coordination — these are emergency signs.
Wingmate Protocol
Watch your group. Cocaine can mask fatigue, dehydration, and physical distress — the person most at risk may not recognise it. Red flags: chest pain, severe headache, irregular heartbeat, extreme agitation, confusion, very high temperature. These require immediate access to the event medical or harm reduction team. Do not wait to see if it passes.

Why this plate exists in the archive.

Cocaine occupies a specific position in harm reduction conversations that makes accurate information particularly important. It is a substance widely encountered across class and cultural contexts, frequently combined with alcohol, and often used in conditions — extended dancing, heat, social intensity — that amplify its physiological risks substantially.

Much public discourse around cocaine operates at two extremes: moral condemnation or casual normalization. Neither framing is pharmacologically useful. This plate exists to provide a third register — precise, evidence-based, non-judgmental — for people who want to understand what the substance actually does, and what the real risk profile looks like under festival and nightlife conditions.

The DAT blockade model chosen for the visual plate reflects the primary and most clinically significant mechanism: the interruption of dopamine reuptake, and the downstream consequences of sustained monoamine elevation. It is not a complete pharmacological account — it is a designed artifact that prioritises transferable understanding over clinical completeness. The archive is an instrument of harm reduction, not a pharmacology textbook.
